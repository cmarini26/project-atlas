# Decision Engine

## Overview

The Decision Engine is the intelligence layer that determines what marketing action Atlas should take next for a given business.

It operates continuously in the background. It reads the Business Brain, evaluates Opportunities, scores and ranks them, commits to Decisions, and produces Recommendations that surface to the user for approval.

The Decision Engine is what separates Atlas from a content generator. A content generator responds to prompts. The Decision Engine acts without being asked.

## Opportunity Scoring

Every Opportunity produced by the Opportunity Engine is scored before it reaches the Decision Engine.

Scoring evaluates four dimensions:

### Relevance

Does this Opportunity align with what this business cares about right now?

Signals:
- Does it involve catalog items that are active and high-priority?
- Does it match the business's stated goals?
- Is it consistent with the channels this business uses?

### Timing

Is now the right moment for this action?

Signals:
- Is there a natural deadline or urgency? (auction ending, seasonal window, days without a campaign)
- Is the business in an active or dormant marketing period?
- Has a similar campaign been run too recently?

### Confidence

How certain is Atlas that this action will work?

Signals:
- Does the business have enough catalog content to support this campaign?
- Has this type of campaign performed well for this business before?
- Is the underlying data fresh and complete?

### Urgency

Will this Opportunity expire if not acted on?

Signals:
- Is there a hard deadline? (auction closing, inventory leaving)
- Is this a fleeting moment? (new arrival, price drop)
- Is the timing advantage lost if Atlas waits another 24 hours?

### Composite Score

Each dimension is scored 0–100. The composite Opportunity Score is a weighted sum:

```
score = (relevance × 0.30) + (timing × 0.25) + (confidence × 0.25) + (urgency × 0.20)
```

Weights are configurable per vertical and adjustable through Learning.

## Confidence Scoring

Confidence is scored separately from the Opportunity Score and exposed independently in every Recommendation.

Confidence reflects Atlas's certainty that a given Decision will produce a good outcome for this business.

Confidence is low when:
- The Digital Twin is new and lacks history
- The catalog data is incomplete or stale
- No similar campaigns have been run before

Confidence is high when:
- The Digital Twin has rich history
- Past campaigns of this type have performed well
- The catalog data is fresh and complete
- The target channel has proven effective

Confidence is always shown to the user. Atlas does not hide uncertainty. A low-confidence Recommendation is still surfaced — but the user sees the score and the reason.

## Explainability

Every Decision must explain itself.

This is a core architectural requirement, not a UI nicety. Explainability is enforced at the data model level — a Decision cannot be persisted without a rationale.

Every Recommendation presented to the user answers four questions:

- **Why now?** What signal or condition triggered this?
- **Why this?** Why this catalog item, campaign type, or angle?
- **Why this channel?** Why social vs. email vs. SMS?
- **Why do we expect this to work?** What evidence or reasoning supports this?

The rationale is generated by the AI layer and stored alongside the Decision. It is not reconstructed at render time — it is captured at decision time.

This serves two purposes:

1. **User trust.** Users approve Recommendations they understand. Opaque AI outputs get rejected or ignored.
2. **Audit trail.** The decision log is a durable record of what Atlas did and why. It feeds Learning and supports debugging.

## Decision Lifecycle

```
Opportunity identified
        ↓
Opportunity scored
        ↓
Opportunity ranked against open queue
        ↓
Decision committed (top-ranked opportunity selected)
        ↓
Rationale generated
        ↓
Recommendation created
        ↓
Campaign prepared (content drafted, assets assembled)
        ↓
Presented for Approval
        ↓
    ┌───────────────────────┐
  Approved               Rejected / Edited
    ↓                        ↓
Execution queued         Feedback captured
    ↓                        ↓
Campaign executed        Learning updated
    ↓
Measurement begins
    ↓
Learning updated
```

## Recommendation Workflow

### Step 1: Opportunity Detection

The Opportunity Engine scans the Business Brain and produces a ranked list of scored Opportunities.

### Step 2: Decision Selection

The Decision Engine selects the highest-scoring Opportunity that has not already been acted on. It checks:

- Has this Opportunity already produced an open (pending approval) Recommendation?
- Has a similar campaign run too recently?
- Does the business have the catalog content needed to execute?

### Step 3: Decision Commitment

The Decision Engine commits to the selected Opportunity. It records:

- The Opportunity reference
- The campaign type selected
- The channel(s) selected
- The rationale (structured JSON)
- The confidence score
- The timestamp

### Step 4: Campaign Preparation

The Campaign Engine receives the committed Decision and prepares the full campaign:

- Campaign strategy and angle
- Target audience
- Call to action
- Content draft(s) for each selected channel
- Suggested schedule

### Step 5: Presentation

The Recommendation is surfaced to the user in the approval interface. The user sees:

- What Atlas wants to do
- Why (the rationale)
- How confident Atlas is
- The prepared content
- The suggested schedule

The user can: Approve, Edit and Approve, or Reject.

### Step 6: Learning

Every outcome — approval, edit, rejection, performance data — is recorded and fed back into the Digital Twin.

The Decision Engine uses accumulated Learning to improve future scoring weights, rationale quality, and channel selection over time.

## Vertical Calibration

The Decision Engine is vertical-aware.

Different business types have different Opportunity patterns. An auction house has urgency-dominated Opportunities (closing soon). A dealership has relevance-dominated Opportunities (new arrivals, long-tail inventory).

Vertical calibration adjusts:
- Scoring weights per vertical
- Opportunity types recognized
- Rationale templates
- Timing heuristics

Calibration is seeded by the knowledge pack for each vertical and refined through Learning.
