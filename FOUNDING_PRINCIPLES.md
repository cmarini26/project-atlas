# Founding Principles

This document is the engineering constitution for Project Atlas. Every engineer and AI agent contributing to this codebase must read it before writing a line of code. When a decision is unclear, return here first.

These principles are not aspirational values. They are engineering constraints that exist because Atlas is a specific kind of system — one that builds trust with small business owners by behaving predictably, explaining itself, and getting smarter over time. Violating these principles produces a system that looks like Atlas but isn't.

---

## 1. Business Before Technology

**Principle:** Every technical decision must be justified by a business outcome, not by technical interest.

**Why it exists:** Atlas is a marketing operating system for small businesses, not a showcase for AI or distributed systems. Small business owners don't care about the architecture. They care whether Atlas helped them sell more vehicles or drive more bids this week. Technology that doesn't serve that outcome is waste.

**How it influences engineering decisions:**
- Do not adopt a pattern, tool, or abstraction because it's interesting. Adopt it because it solves a problem the business has right now or will have at a known scale.
- When evaluating two approaches, the simpler one wins unless there is a concrete, near-term reason the complex one is necessary.
- Features are scoped to the smallest thing that proves the loop works. The MVP proves one workflow end-to-end. Extensions come after the core is proven.

**Examples:**
- pgvector is not wired up in MVP because no feature requires semantic search yet. The interface is defined. The implementation waits.
- Full event sourcing is not used because append-only tables give the audit properties we need without the infrastructure burden. If that changes, the domain event table is already designed.
- The Opportunity Engine runs rule-based detectors first because they are fast, cheap, and deterministic. The AI-based analyst runs second, only to catch what rules miss.

## 2. AI Is Infrastructure, Not the Product

**Principle:** AI is how Atlas works internally. It is never what the user interacts with.

**Why it exists:** A system where users talk to AI is a chatbot. Atlas is not a chatbot. The product is the Digital Twin — a living model of the business that accumulates intelligence over time. The product is the Recommendation — a prepared campaign waiting for approval. AI is the engine inside these things, invisible to the user.

If AI were the product, swapping models would be a product change. In Atlas, swapping models is a config change.

**How it influences engineering decisions:**
- `AiProvider` is an interface. No controller, job, listener, model, or service outside `app/Services/Analyst/` calls it directly.
- Prompt engineering is treated like code: versioned, reviewed, tested with fixtures, stored in typed classes.
- The user never sees a prompt, a token count, or a raw model response. They see a Recommendation with a rationale.
- When a better model ships, it is swapped in configuration. Nothing else changes.

**Examples:**
- The four `why_*` rationale fields are generated by AI but stored as structured data on the `Decision` record. The user reads a clear explanation written in plain language, not a raw model output.
- `ContentGenerationAnalyst` uses a typed `SocialContentPrompt` with a versioned schema. The Instagram post the user sees is a `ContentAsset` record, not a streamed AI response.
- `FakeAiProvider` is used in every test. No test calls a real AI provider. AI is infrastructure — it can be mocked like any other infrastructure.

## 3. Every Recommendation Must Be Explainable

**Principle:** Atlas must always be able to answer: why now, why this, why this channel, why do we expect this to work.

**Why it exists:** Users will not approve what they don't understand. Opacity destroys trust. A recommendation that cannot explain itself is a guess — and Atlas is not a guessing tool, it is an intelligence system. Explainability is also how Atlas earns the right to act more autonomously over time.

**How it influences engineering decisions:**
- Explainability is enforced at the data model level, not just the UI. A `Decision` record cannot be persisted without all four rationale fields (`why_now`, `why_this`, `why_channel`, `why_works`) and a structured `expected_impact`. This is validated in `DecisionService`, not just a form request.
- `prompt_name` and `prompt_version` are stored on every AI-produced record: Facts, Knowledge entries, Decisions, ContentAssets. This creates a full audit trail from output back to the prompt that produced it.
- Confidence scores are always surfaced. Atlas does not hide uncertainty. A low-confidence recommendation is shown with its score and the reason for low confidence.

**Examples:**
- `RationaleGenerationAnalyst` produces `{why_now, why_this, why_channel, why_works, expected_impact}` as structured JSON. If any key is missing or empty, `RationaleGenerationFailedException` is thrown and the Decision is not committed.
- A CBB Auctions urgency recommendation explains: "12 auctions close in 48 hours — this window expires tomorrow. Urgency-framed posts see 2–3× higher click-through than standard inventory posts."
- When a user rejects a Recommendation, the rejection is recorded as a Learning with a `notes` field. That Learning eventually informs why Atlas doesn't make the same recommendation next time.

## 4. Humans Approve External Actions

**Principle:** No content is published, no message is sent, and no external action is taken without explicit human approval.

**Why it exists:** Atlas has access to a business's brand, audience, and publishing channels. A mistake published in public costs more than the campaign was worth. Trust must be earned before autonomy is extended. The approval gate is not a limitation — it is the product. "A smart recommendation waiting for approval" is the mantra.

**How it influences engineering decisions:**
- No `Execution` record transitions to `executing` without an `Approval` record in state `approved` or `edited_and_approved`. This is enforced in `ExecutionService`, not just the UI.
- Publishing logic is not built until the approval workflow is proven. `Execution` records are created in `queued` status in MVP — they stay there until publishing is deliberately built and tested.
- The `Approval` model is append-only. If a user changes their mind, a new `Approval` is created. The original is never modified.
- Every `Approval` stores `user_id` and `acted_at`. The audit trail is complete and permanent.

**Examples:**
- A `CampaignApproved` event fires only after an `Approval` record exists. `ExecutionService` checks for a valid Approval before processing — it does not trust status fields alone.
- Even when Atlas is 99% confident in a recommendation, the approve button still exists. Confidence informs the UI (e.g., "Atlas is highly confident"), not the gate.
- `Approval.edits` captures what the user changed when they edit content before approving. This diff feeds Learning — Atlas understands what its output needed in order to be acceptable.

## 5. Generic Platform, Specialized Extensions

**Principle:** Core Atlas is built around generic concepts. Vertical-specific behavior is expressed through configuration, metadata, and registered extensions — not new tables, models, or modules.

**Why it exists:** Atlas serves comic book auction houses, exotic car dealers, and eventually dozens of other verticals. If the platform is built around any one vertical, adding the next vertical requires rewriting the core. Generic primitives make every new vertical a configuration problem, not an engineering problem.

**How it influences engineering decisions:**
- Use `CatalogItem` not `Vehicle`, `Listing`, or `ComicBook`. Vertical-specific fields go in `catalog_items.metadata` (JSON), validated against `catalogs.item_schema`.
- Use `subject_type` / `subject_id` polymorphic fields on `Opportunity` rather than `catalog_item_id`. An Opportunity can be about a CatalogItem, a Catalog, or a Company.
- `OpportunityDetector` classes are registered per vertical. Adding a new vertical means registering new detectors, not modifying the engine.
- Avoid naming anything in the core domain after a specific vertical. If a name only makes sense for one business type, it belongs in an extension.

**Examples:**
- CBB Auctions and exotic dealers both use `catalog_items`. The `metadata` field carries `{grade, key_issue, auction_end_at}` for comics and `{make, model, year, mileage, vin}` for vehicles. One table. One model. One set of services.
- `UrgencyDetector` fires for CBB (auction closing) and could fire for a car dealer (price drop, days-in-inventory threshold) — same detector, different metadata signals.
- A restaurant vertical can be added by defining an `item_schema` for menu items, registering a `SeasonalMenuDetector`, and adding restaurant-aware prompt context. No new tables, no new models.

## 6. Specifications Drive Code

**Principle:** Write the spec before writing the code. The spec is the contract. Code is the implementation of the contract.

**Why it exists:** In an AI-assisted development environment, code is written faster than it used to be. That speed is valuable. It is also dangerous without discipline — fast code written without a shared spec produces a system where every module makes different assumptions about the same domain concepts. Specs make every contributor, human or AI, build the same system.

**How it influences engineering decisions:**
- Before writing a migration, read `specs/core/domain-model.md`. If the entity isn't defined there, define it first.
- Before writing a service, read `docs/technical/Architecture.md`. If the module structure or event chain isn't clear, clarify it in the spec before writing code.
- When a spec and the code conflict, treat the spec as correct until a deliberate decision is made to change it — then update the spec first.
- AI agents working on this codebase are given specs, not just descriptions. The spec is what gets passed as context.

**Examples:**
- Every table in the database corresponds to an entity in `specs/core/domain-model.md` with defined fields, relationships, lifecycle states, and Laravel notes. No migration is written from scratch.
- `specs/product/mvp-workflow.md` defines 13 steps with exact records, events, and acceptance criteria before a single controller is built. The controller is an implementation of Step 3 — not a design decision made in the moment.
- When a new Analyst is needed, its input/output types, prompt summary, and JSON schema are defined in `docs/technical/AI.md` before the class is written.

## 7. Event-Driven Where It Adds Value

**Principle:** Use domain events to decouple modules and enable async processing. Do not use events as a substitute for direct method calls within a module.

**Why it exists:** Events are powerful. They are also indirection. Overuse produces a system where following the execution path requires reading a dozen listener registrations before you understand what happens when a button is clicked. Use events where they genuinely decouple things that should not know about each other. Use direct calls everywhere else.

**How it influences engineering decisions:**
- Events cross module boundaries. `ObservationRecorded` is fired by Observatory so Analyst can respond without Observatory knowing about Analyst. That is the right use.
- Direct service calls happen within a module. `CampaignPreparationService` can call `ContentGenerationService` in sequence — they are part of the same preparation flow, and the coupling is appropriate.
- Events are past-tense and describe what happened: `ObservationRecorded`, not `RecordObservation`. Listeners do the work; events do not contain behavior.
- Listeners are thin: one precondition check, one service call or job dispatch, return. If a listener is making decisions, that logic belongs in a service.

**Examples:**
- `DigitalTwinActivated` fires once when a Digital Twin transitions from `initializing` to `active`. Its listener dispatches `DetectOpportunities`. Observatory, Analyst, and Opportunity modules are all decoupled through this chain.
- `ProcessObservation` calls `FactExtractionAnalyst::analyze()` directly — not through an event — because Fact extraction is a direct consequence of Observation processing, within the same job.
- `RecommendationApproved` fires from `ApprovalService`. Multiple listeners respond: one creates Executions, one records a Learning. Two independent concerns triggered by one event — the correct use.

## 8. Test Business Behavior, Not Frameworks

**Principle:** Tests assert that the business loop works correctly. They do not assert that Laravel, Eloquent, or the queue system behaves as documented.

**Why it exists:** A test suite full of database assertions and mock verifications gives the impression of coverage while missing the actual risk. The risk in Atlas is not that `$fact->save()` fails — it's that the wrong Facts are extracted, the wrong Opportunity is selected, or the Decision rationale is incomplete. Tests must be written to catch those failures.

**How it influences engineering decisions:**
- Test the outcome of a service call, not the internal calls it makes. Assert that `FactExtractionAnalyst::analyze()` produces Facts with correct keys and types — not that it called `AiProvider::complete()` once.
- Use `FakeAiProvider` with fixture JSON responses. Every Analyst test runs against a real fixture response, exercising the full parse → validate → hydrate → persist path without calling a real provider.
- Test failure paths: malformed AI response, schema validation failure, missing rationale keys. These are the cases that corrupt the loop.
- Do not write tests that mock Eloquent methods. If persistence behavior needs testing, use a real database (SQLite in-memory or a test PostgreSQL instance).

**Examples:**
- An `ApprovalService::approve()` test asserts: Recommendation status is `approved`, Decision status is `approved`, one Execution record per ContentAsset is created, one Learning record is created. It does not assert that `$recommendation->update()` was called.
- A `DecisionEngine::evaluate()` test with two open Opportunities asserts that the higher-scoring one is selected and the correct guards are applied — not that the right SQL query was executed.
- A `RationaleGenerationAnalyst` test queues a fixture response missing the `why_now` key and asserts that `RationaleGenerationFailedException` is thrown and no Decision is persisted.

## 9. Small, Observable, Maintainable Components

**Principle:** Every component does one thing. Every meaningful state transition is logged. The system's behavior at any moment must be diagnosable without reading application code.

**Why it exists:** The Atlas loop spans 13 steps, 6 AI calls, and a chain of jobs and events. When something goes wrong — a recommendation isn't surfacing, a Decision isn't being committed — the engineer diagnosing it should be able to trace exactly what happened by reading the database and the logs. This only works if every component is small enough to have a clear single responsibility and every transition is observable.

**How it influences engineering decisions:**
- Jobs orchestrate. Services decide. Models persist. Nothing crosses that boundary.
- Every job that dispatches other jobs logs what it dispatched and why.
- Every AI call stores the prompt version that produced the output. If a Knowledge entry is wrong, you can identify which prompt version caused it.
- `status` fields on every entity (`pending`, `processing`, `processed`, `open`, `selected`, `recommended`, `approved`) are not UI decoration — they are the observable record of where each entity is in the loop.
- Services raise named exceptions, not generic ones. `RationaleGenerationFailedException` is diagnosable. `Exception: something went wrong` is not.

**Examples:**
- `CommitDecision` job calls `DecisionEngine::evaluate()`. It does not contain guard logic. If a Decision is not committed, the engine explains why in a log entry (`no_opportunities_passed_guards`, `all_opportunities_in_cooldown`).
- `ProcessObservation` sets `observation.status = 'processing'` before the AI call and `'processed'` after. If a job fails mid-flight, the status reveals exactly where it stopped.
- `FactExtractionAnalyst` stores `prompt_name: "FactExtractionPrompt"` and `prompt_version: "1.1"` on every Fact it creates. When the prompt is updated, facts produced by the new version are immediately distinguishable from older ones.

## 10. Atlas Must Learn Over Time

**Principle:** Every approval, rejection, edit, and campaign outcome is recorded and eventually incorporated into the Business Brain. The system must be measurably smarter after 90 days than it was at setup.

**Why it exists:** The Digital Twin is Atlas's competitive moat. It only becomes a moat if it actually accumulates intelligence. A system that runs the loop but doesn't record what happened — what the user approved, what they changed, what drove results — is a content generator, not an intelligence system. Learnings are not a nice-to-have. They are what justifies the Digital Twin's existence.

**How it influences engineering decisions:**
- Every `Approval`, `Rejection`, and `ExecutionCompleted` event creates a `Learning` record. This is not optional behavior that can be added later — it is part of the core event listeners.
- `Learning.applied_at` tracks when the learning was incorporated into the Business Brain. Unapplied Learnings are visible and queryable. The `ApplyLearnings` job processes them on a schedule.
- Aggregate patterns across Learnings eventually become Knowledge entries, which influence Opportunity scoring. This is the feedback loop that makes Atlas improve.
- `prompt_version` on AI-produced records enables retrospective performance analysis: did version 1.1 of the Rationale prompt produce higher approval rates than 1.0? The data is there to answer that.

**Examples:**
- A user rejects a recommendation because the channel was wrong. `Learning` records `signal: "user_rejected_channel"`, `value: {channel: "email", campaign_type: "urgency"}`. After enough of these signals, Atlas learns not to recommend email for urgency campaigns for this company.
- A user edits a ContentAsset's body before approving. `Approval.edits` captures the diff. Over time, these edits reveal patterns: this company's owner always adds a price, always removes hashtags. Future content generation incorporates these preferences.
- A Knowledge entry states "no campaigns in 14 days — audience disengagement risk." After the campaign runs and engagement spikes, an `ExecutionCompleted` Learning records the outcome. The next synthesis cycle updates the Knowledge: "campaigns after 14-day gaps produce above-average engagement for this company."

## Summary

| # | Principle | The test |
|---|-----------|----------|
| 1 | Business Before Technology | Can you name the business outcome this decision serves? |
| 2 | AI Is Infrastructure | Could you swap the AI provider without changing any business logic? |
| 3 | Every Recommendation Is Explainable | Can you show a user exactly why Atlas made this recommendation, in plain language? |
| 4 | Humans Approve External Actions | Is there an Approval record in the database before any content goes anywhere? |
| 5 | Generic Platform, Specialized Extensions | Would this code still work if the vertical changed from cars to comics? |
| 6 | Specifications Drive Code | Does a spec exist before this code was written? |
| 7 | Event-Driven Where It Adds Value | Does this event genuinely decouple two things that shouldn't know about each other? |
| 8 | Test Business Behavior | Does this test fail if the business logic is wrong, not just if the framework misbehaves? |
| 9 | Small, Observable, Maintainable | Can you determine what this component did by reading the database and logs alone? |
| 10 | Atlas Must Learn | Is this outcome recorded as a Learning that will make the next recommendation better? |

---

*These principles were established at the founding of Project Atlas. They may be refined as the system matures, but they may not be abandoned. Any proposed change to a principle requires explicit discussion and a record of the reasoning.*
